## The surface argument

<!-- Core thesis: a dashboard is a Level 1-2 surface (human reads, interprets, decides,
acts). Pointing a Level 4-8 agent at a problem and getting back a chart re-inserts the
human bottleneck. The anti-pattern is the level mismatch, not the dashboard itself. -->

## A monument to the pre-agent constraint

<!-- Dashboards exist because compute couldn't reason over data, so we offloaded reasoning
to human eyeballs. Agents remove that constraint. -->

## From pull to push-on-exception

<!-- The interface shifts from scanning green tiles to a brief: "here are the 3 things that
need you; here's what I handled for the other 40." -->

## Scanning is not a workflow

<!-- Level 5: a real workflow professionalizes output, closes the loop, logs actions, and
escalates only exceptions. A dashboard makes the human the integration layer. -->

## "Generate me a dashboard" is a tell

<!-- Usually means the requester hasn't decided what action the data should drive. Surface
the decision, not the display. -->

## The new surfaces of full-capability agents

<!-- Conversational query (ask, don't scan); approval queues (agent proposes, you approve);
silent remediation with an audit trail (agent already fixed it). Dashboards are what you
build when you don't trust any of those yet. -->

## Cost and trust

<!-- Straight from the Eight Levels: higher levels demand more trust and tokens. A dashboard
is a cheap trust substitute. Fine as scaffolding, dangerous as the destination. -->

## Steelman: when dashboards still earn their place
